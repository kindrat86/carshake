The damage risk for exotic car rental companies

Exotic car rental companies face the highest per-vehicle damage risk in the auto industry. A single scratch on a Lamborghini hood can cost $3,000-8,000 to repair, and a curbed wheel on a Ferrari can run $1,500-4,000 per wheel. Customers often have limited experience with supercars, increasing the likelihood of curb damage, parking lot incidents, and interior wear.

Without rigorous condition documentation, exotic rental disputes become high-stakes legal battles. Customers deny responsibility, credit card dispute processes drag on for months, and the rental company is left absorbing five-figure repair bills.

How CarShake protects exotic car rental companies

CarShake documents every vehicle's condition at pickup and return with cryptographic precision. For exotic rentals where each scratch matters, the timestamped photo evidence makes damage attribution clear-cut and significantly improves the success rate of credit card disputes and insurance claims.

The protocol takes about 90 seconds:

  1. Scan before handover: Walk around the vehicle capturing all exterior panels, wheels, and interior. CarShake timestamps and geolocates every photo.
  2. QR receipt at handover: Generate a QR code that the customer, passenger, or driver scans, creating a cryptographic receipt linking them to the documented condition.
  3. Scan at return: When the vehicle comes back, repeat the scan. Any new damage is flagged and attributable to the specific custody period.

Can customers dispute CarShake documentation?

CarShake photos include cryptographic timestamps and geolocation data that are extremely difficult to forge. Credit card companies and courts accept this documentation as strong evidence of vehicle condition at a specific time and place, which significantly reduces successful disputes.

Does this work with my existing rental or fleet management software?

What if a customer refuses to scan the QR code?

The QR scan is optional. Your pre-handover photos are the critical evidence. If a customer won't scan the QR, you still have timestamped proof of the vehicle's condition before they took possession, which is usually sufficient for dispute resolution.
