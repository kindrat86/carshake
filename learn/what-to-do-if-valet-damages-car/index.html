What to Do If a Valet Damages Your Car

step-by-step guide — evidence collection, documentation, and claim filing

Discovering a fresh dent, scratch, or cracked wheel when the valet returns your car is infuriating, but the actions you take in the first 30 minutes determine whether you get paid. Most owners fail claims not because they were wrong, but because they left the lot, repaired the damage, or relied on memory instead of documentation. This guide walks through the exact sequence that converts a damaged car into a settled check.

1. Do not move the car

The single most important step is also the one most owners skip. If the valet returns the car with damage you did not cause, do not pull out of the driveway, do not drive home, do not "deal with it tomorrow." Leaving the premises gives the operator a built-in defense: the damage could have happened anywhere. Stop the car where it is, leave it parked, and go directly to the valet stand.

2. Notify the manager in writing

Ask to speak with the manager on duty, not the attendant. State clearly: "The car was returned with damage that was not present when I handed over the keys." Ask for a written incident report. Take a photo of the report. If the manager refuses, document the refusal in writing and capture the names of any witnesses.

3. Capture the evidence

Photograph the damage from multiple angles, including wide shots that show the car's location relative to lot signage and nearby vehicles. Run a CarShake post-retrieval scan so the new damage is timestamped and compared against your pre-handover scan. If you do not have a pre-handover scan, request surveillance footage from the venue immediately — most systems overwrite every 7 to 30 days.

4. Get the valet's insurance information

Ask for the valet company's legal name, insurance carrier, and policy number. Independent operators are frequently uninsured or underinsured. If they refuse to provide insurance details, that refusal becomes evidence in your favor — note it and photograph the valet license or business permit displayed at the stand.

5. File a police report if damage exceeds your deductible

For damage above roughly $500 to $1,000, file a police report. Many jurisdictions classify valet damage as a property incident and create a record number you can cite in your insurance claim. The report also anchors the time and location of the damage, which becomes important if the valet's insurer disputes liability.

6. Open parallel insurance claims

File with the valet's insurer and with your own auto insurer under collision coverage. Your insurer typically pays faster and then pursues subrogation against the valet. Provide both carriers with the same evidence package: timestamped photos, the incident report, two repair estimates, and the CarShake pre- and post-handover scans.

7. Get two independent repair estimates

Insurers will pressure you toward their preferred network shops, which routinely under-estimate damage. Get written estimates from two independent licensed body shops. The difference between an insurer-preferred estimate and an independent one is often 30 to 50 percent.

8. Escalate if stalled

If the valet's insurer has not responded within 30 days, file a complaint with your state's department of insurance — this is the single fastest way to unstick a stalled claim. For damage above $5,000, an attorney demand letter typically produces a settlement within two weeks.

FAQ

Can I still claim if I already drove the car home? Yes, but it is much harder. Send written notice within 24 hours and request surveillance footage immediately.

What if the valet company has no insurance? Pursue the venue that hired them under a theory of vicarious liability, then file with your own insurer for subrogation.

Should I accept their first offer? Rarely. Initial offers average 40 to 60 percent of the actual repair cost. Counter with your independent estimates.
