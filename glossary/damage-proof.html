Damage proof: definition

Damage proof is timestamped, photographic evidence of a vehicle's condition at a specific moment, strong enough that both parties to a dispute — and if necessary an insurer or court — can rely on it. A photo alone is not damage proof; a photo with a trusted timestamp, location, integrity guarantee, and the other party's confirmation is.

The four elements of damage proof

Evidence about vehicle condition gets attacked in predictable ways, so useful damage proof answers four questions in advance:

  1. What? Clear photographs covering the whole vehicle — CarShake's guided scan captures 8 angles so no panel is missing when it matters.
  2. When? A timestamp the parties cannot alter. Phone-gallery EXIF data can be edited; CarShake applies a server-side timestamp at capture.
  3. Where and which record? A GPS stamp ties the scan to the handover location, and a SHA-256 hash of each photo proves the file was not modified or swapped afterward.
  4. Says who? The strongest element: the other party confirms the record at handover via QR handshake. Evidence the opposing side already acknowledged is very hard to argue with.

Why gallery photos fall short

Most drivers who “document” their car take a few photos before handing over the keys. In a dispute, those photos face three objections: the date can be questioned, the coverage is partial, and the other party never saw them. That is the difference between having photos and having proof.

Frequently asked questions

Is damage proof legally binding?

Damage proof is evidence, not a contract. How much weight it carries depends on the venue, but evidence with trusted timestamps and the other party's confirmation is far harder to rebut than loose photos.

What makes a timestamp trustworthy?

It must come from a source neither party controls. CarShake stamps captures server-side, and each photo's SHA-256 hash proves it wasn't altered afterward.
