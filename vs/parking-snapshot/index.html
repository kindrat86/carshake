CarShake vs Parking Snapshot

A manual photo app vs. a handover protocol with court-admissible timestamps and QR receipts.

"Parking Snapshot" describes a category of lightweight parking apps — and the broader habit of just opening your phone's camera before you walk away from a parked car — that lets you snap a few photos of your vehicle and the surrounding stall. The impulse is right: take a picture before you leave so you have something to point at later. The execution, though, leaves three evidentiary gaps that matter the moment a dispute actually happens.

First, a phone photo carries a timestamp the photographer controls and metadata that can be edited or stripped. Second, a photo album documents condition but not custody — it does not tie a specific image to a specific moment of key transfer. Third, a photo is one-sided: the valet never saw it, never confirmed it, and can reasonably argue the damage was already there when the photo was taken. CarShake exists to close those three gaps.

When to choose CarShake

Choose CarShake when the photo matters as evidence, not as a memory. The difference between "I took a picture of my bumper" and "I have a court-admissible timestamped record with a mutually-confirmed QR handover receipt" is the difference between a photo a valet operator can dismiss and one they have to engage with. The guided 8-angle AI scan also removes the most common failure mode of DIY photography: forgetting to capture the angle where the eventual damage actually appears.

CarShake is also the better choice when the handover involves a third party you do not know — a valet, a rental agent, a mechanic, a stranger at a concert parking drop-off. The QR receipt creates a shared artifact both parties acknowledged, which is what converts a one-sided photo into a two-sided record. Without that confirmation step, a photo is just a claim.

When Parking Snapshot / phone photos might be better

Phone photos win when the goal is casual documentation, not evidence. If you are parking in your own driveway, photographing a fender-bender at the scene for insurance, or just tracking your car's wear over time, your phone's camera is the right tool. There is no setup, no app, no protocol — and for those use cases, none is needed.

Photos also win on cost. CarShake's free tier covers three scans per month, which is plenty for most drivers, but if you are documenting a vehicle dozens of times a week — a detailer logging condition before and after each job, for example — your phone's camera roll may genuinely be the simpler workflow, provided you accept the evidentiary trade-offs.

Frequently asked questions

Why can't I just use my phone's camera?

You can, and CarShake does not replace casual photography. The limitation is evidentiary: phone photos have editable metadata, do not prove custody, and have not been confirmed by the other party. When those things matter — a damage dispute, a small-claims case, an insurance claim — the gaps are exactly what a valet operator will exploit.

Does CarShake's timestamp really hold up in court?

CarShake stamps every scan with an immutable server-side timestamp and geolocation — the properties courts and insurers look for in contemporaneous records. Whether a specific court admits it always depends on jurisdiction and facts, but the design goal is admissibility.

What does the QR handover receipt actually do?

It creates a shared record that both parties (driver and valet/agent) have acknowledged the same vehicle condition at the same moment. That mutual confirmation is what makes the evidence hard to dismiss later.

Is the free tier enough for most drivers?

Yes. Three scans per month covers typical valet and rental usage. Shield+ ($2.97/month) is for frequent travelers, rideshare drivers, and anyone who hands their car over more than a few times a month.
