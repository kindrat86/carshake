What to Do When a Valet Damages Your Car — The Complete 2026 Guide

BLUF: Do not leave the scene. Photograph the damage immediately, demand an incident report, and document every interaction. Without timestamped before-state evidence, approximately 70% of valet damage claims fail. The valet's defense — "it was already like that" — is nearly impossible to refute unless you have a pre-handover scan. This guide walks through exactly what to do in the first 15 minutes, the first 24 hours, and the following days to maximize your chance of full compensation.

The First 15 Minutes: What to Do at the Scene

The 15 minutes after you discover damage are the most important. Every action you take — or fail to take — determines whether your claim succeeds.

  1. Do not leave the premises. Once you drive away, the valet can claim the damage happened after you left. Stay parked where you are.
  2. Photograph the damage immediately. Take close-up and wide-angle photos. Capture the damage from multiple angles. If you have a CarShake pre-handover scan, open the app and compare the before/after — the AI comparison showing the damage wasn't in the pre-scan is your single strongest piece of evidence.
  3. Photograph the surroundings. Include the valet stand, the exact location where your car was returned, any security cameras, lighting conditions, and the valet ticket in your photos.
  4. Ask for the valet manager on duty. Do not argue with the attendant — speak to the manager. State calmly: "I handed you my car in undamaged condition. I have timestamped photographic evidence. There is new damage that was not present at drop-off. I need an incident report filed immediately."
  5. Get the manager's name, phone number, and email. Also note the valet company's name if different from the venue.
  6. Do not accept cash or an informal offer. If the valet manager offers to pay out of pocket, politely decline. Accepting cash may waive your right to file a formal claim. Insist on a written acknowledgment.

The First 24 Hours: Document Everything

The evidence you collect in the first 24 hours is what makes or breaks your claim.

  • Write down everything while it's fresh. Exact time of drop-off and pickup, name of the valet attendant, description of the valet manager's response, any witnesses. Memory fades — write it down now.
  • Get a repair estimate. Visit a body shop within 24–48 hours for a written estimate. 1–2 estimates strengthen your claim. The estimate is what establishes the dollar amount of your demand.
  • Send a written notice to the valet company. Email the manager summarizing the incident, attaching your photos, and stating your intent to pursue the claim. This creates a paper trail. Use our free demand letter generator to produce a formal letter citing bailment law.
  • Contact your insurance company. Even if you intend to pursue the valet company, notify your insurer. Your policy may cover the repair and then subrogate (pursue reimbursement) from the valet company's insurer.
  • Check for security cameras. Ask the venue if they have surveillance footage of the valet area. Many venues retain footage for 7–30 days. Request it in writing immediately.

The Law: How Bailment Protects You

When you hand your car keys to a valet, you create a bailment — a legal relationship recognized in all 50 states. Here's what that means in practice:

  • You (the bailor) transfer temporary custody of your vehicle.
  • The valet (the bailee) accepts that custody and owes you a duty of reasonable care — they must return the car in the same condition.
  • When damage occurs, the law presumes the bailee was negligent unless they can prove otherwise.
  • But here's the catch: you must first prove that the damage occurred during the bailment. Without a documented pre-handover condition, that's nearly impossible.

When the Valet Denies Responsibility

The most common valet response: "How do we know it wasn't already like that?" This is effective because the burden of proof falls on you — and most drivers have no before-state evidence.

Your response depends on what evidence you have:

  • If you have a CarShake pre-handover scan: Show the before/after comparison. The timestamps prove the damage occurred during their custody. This typically resolves the claim immediately — most valet managers will settle when presented with timestamped evidence they cannot refute.
  • If you have phone photos from before the handover: Less strong but still valuable. Phone photos lack verified timestamps and GPS, but combined with the valet ticket they establish a timeline.
  • If you have no before-state evidence: Your claim is significantly weaker but not impossible. The valet ticket, scene photos, witness statements, and security footage can still support your case. File the incident report and send a demand letter — some valet companies settle even without perfect evidence to avoid negative reviews and legal costs.

Escalation: Small Claims Court and Beyond

If the valet company denies your claim or ignores your demand letter:

  1. Small claims court is your primary remedy. Limits range from $3,500 (Arizona) to $25,000 (Tennessee). Most valet damage falls within these limits. You do not need a lawyer. Bring your photos, the valet ticket, repair estimates, and any correspondence. A well-documented pre-handover scan is often the deciding piece of evidence.
  2. File a complaint with the Better Business Bureau and your state's Attorney General consumer protection division. These complaints create a public record and often motivate settlement.
  3. Contact the venue (hotel, restaurant) if the valet is a third-party operator. The venue's management may pressure the valet company to resolve the claim to protect their reputation.
  4. Your insurance company's subrogation department can pursue the valet company's insurer after paying your claim. This is often the path of least resistance if you have comprehensive coverage.
