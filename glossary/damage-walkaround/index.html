Damage Walkaround

A systematic visual inspection of a vehicle's exterior to identify and document existing damage.

In depth

The damage walkaround is the most important step in any vehicle handover. It must be conducted in good lighting, follow a consistent pattern (typically clockwise from driver's front), and capture all pre-existing damage.

Why damage walkaround matters in valet and damage disputes

Damage Walkaround is one of those terms that sounds abstract until you are standing in a hotel driveway arguing with a valet manager about a dent that was not there when you handed over the keys. Then it becomes the single most important word in the conversation. In the valet and damage-dispute context, damage walkaround is the difference between a claim you can prove and a claim you have to walk away from.

The core problem in almost every vehicle damage dispute is custody. While someone else has your car — a valet, a rental company, a dealer service department, a body shop — any damage that appears during that window is presumptively theirs. But "presumptively" only helps you if you can prove the condition of the car at the moment you handed it over and at the moment you got it back. That proof is exactly what damage walkaround describes in practice, and exactly what gets absent when drivers lose disputes they should have won.

How damage walkaround shows up in real disputes

  • The handover moment: this is when damage walkaround is established — or fails to be established. A proper handover includes a documented, time-stamped, witnessed record of the vehicle condition at the instant of transfer.
  • The denial: without that record, the operator almost always claims the damage was pre-existing or occurred after you left. Damage walkaround is the language you use (and the evidence you produce) to defeat that denial.
  • The resolution: when damage walkaround is properly documented, disputes that would have dragged on for weeks tend to resolve in a single conversation. Operators know what a court-admissible before/after diff looks like.
