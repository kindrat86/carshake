How to Prove Valet Damage to Your Car

3-step protocol: scan before valet, QR handover receipt, photograph after

Valet damage claims succeed or fail on one thing: proof. Without a verifiable record of your car's condition before you handed over the keys, the valet company's insurer will almost always argue the damage was pre-existing — and absent hard evidence, they usually win. This guide explains exactly what evidence holds up in court, the three-step protocol that produces it, and how to assemble a damage file that gets you paid.

Step 1: Document condition before handover

Before the valet drives off, walk around the car and capture a complete condition record. Aim for at least eight photos: one straight-on shot of each side (front, rear, left, right) and one close-up of each corner showing the wheels and bumper caps. Note any existing damage in the same shots so it cannot be later blamed on you. CarShake's pre-handover scan automates this: a single guided pass produces a timestamped, geo-located set of images that cannot be edited after capture.

The critical detail most owners miss is the timestamp. Smartphone photos carry EXIF metadata, but that metadata is trivially editable. To hold up against a defense attorney, the timestamp must come from a neutral third party — exactly what CarShake provides by stamping the scan with a server-side time record that is tamper-evident.

Step 2: Get a QR handover receipt

The moment the valet accepts the keys is the moment legal custody transfers. A paper ticket is meaningless evidence because it carries no condition record and no verifiable timestamp. A QR handover receipt from CarShake documents the exact moment of transfer and links to the pre-handover scan. This receipt becomes the keystone of your evidence: any damage present in the post-retrieval scan but absent from the pre-handover scan must have occurred during the valet's custody.

Step 3: Document condition after retrieval

When the valet returns the car, do not drive away. Walk around the vehicle in the same order and capture the same eight angles plus close-ups of any new damage. The post-retrieval scan in CarShake automatically compares against the pre-handover scan and flags differences. If you find damage, notify the manager on duty immediately, in writing, before leaving the premises.

What courts and insurers actually accept

  • Timestamped photos with neutral, verifiable time records (CarShake scans, not edited EXIF)
  • A written incident report signed by the venue manager
  • Two independent repair estimates from licensed body shops
  • Surveillance footage requests sent within 24 hours (most venues overwrite footage after 7 to 30 days)
  • The valet's repair order, rental agreement, or parking contract

Common proof failures

Most owners lose valet claims not because they were wrong about who caused the damage, but because they cannot prove the damage did not exist before. Driving the car off the lot, repairing the damage before an adjuster sees it, and relying on photos with no timestamp are the three failure modes CarShake is designed to eliminate.

FAQ

Do smartphone photos hold up in court? Sometimes, but only if the metadata has not been altered. Server-side timestamps from CarShake are far stronger evidence.

What if I forgot to scan before handing over? Without a pre-handover record, the claim becomes much harder. Surveillance footage and witness statements become your fallback.

How long should I keep the records? At least two years, which covers most state statutes of limitations for property damage claims.
