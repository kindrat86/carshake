What do I do if a valet damages my car?

File a claim with the valet company within 24 hours, present timestamped pre-damage proof (CarShake scan), request their insurance info, and file a police report if the damage exceeds your deductible.

Detailed answer

If a valet damages your car, the actions you take in the first 30 minutes determine whether you get paid. The single most important step is also the one most owners skip: do not drive away from the lot. Once you leave the premises, the operator has a built-in defense that the damage could have happened anywhere. Stop the car where it is, walk to the valet stand, and ask to speak with the manager on duty.

Once the manager is engaged, file a written incident report immediately. Take a photo of the report. Ask for the valet company's legal name, insurance carrier, and policy number. Independent operators are often uninsured or underinsured, which is why your pre-handover documentation matters so much. Photograph the damage from multiple angles, including wide shots showing the car's location relative to lot signage. If you have a CarShake pre-handover scan, run a post-retrieval scan immediately so the new damage is timestamped and compared against your "before" record.

Within 24 hours, open parallel insurance claims with the valet's carrier and your own auto insurer under collision coverage. Provide both carriers with the same evidence package: timestamped pre- and post-handover scans, the incident report, two independent repair estimates, and the police report (filed if damage exceeds your deductible). For damage above $500 to $1,000, file a police report — it anchors the time and location of the damage and is required by some insurers. If the valet's insurer stalls past 30 days, file a complaint with your state's department of insurance, which is the single fastest way to unstick a stalled claim.

Step-by-step checklist

  1. Stop the car where it was returned. Do not drive off the lot.
  2. Walk to the valet stand and ask for the manager on duty.
  3. File a written incident report. Photograph the report.
  4. Get the valet company's legal name, insurance carrier, and policy number.
  5. Photograph the damage from multiple angles, including wide context shots.
  6. Run a CarShake post-retrieval scan and compare to your pre-handover scan.
  7. File a police report if damage exceeds your deductible.
  8. Open parallel claims with the valet's insurer and your own within 24 hours.
  9. Obtain two independent repair estimates from licensed body shops.
  10. Send a written demand letter with a 14-day response deadline.
  11. Escalate to your state's department of insurance if the claim stalls past 30 days.

Related questions

What if I already drove the car home?

You can still file, but the claim is much harder. Send written notice within 24 hours, request surveillance footage from the venue immediately (most overwrite within 7 to 30 days), and rely on any timestamped pre-handover scan you captured.

What if the valet company has no insurance?

Pursue the venue that hired the valet under a theory of vicarious liability, then file with your own auto insurer for subrogation. Your insurer will pursue the operator and the venue for recovery.

Should I accept their first offer?

Rarely. Initial settlement offers average 40 to 60 percent of the actual repair cost. Counter with your independent estimates and the CarShake evidence package.

When should I hire an attorney?

For damage above $5,000, disputes involving total-loss valuations, or claims involving personal injury. An attorney demand letter typically produces a settlement within two weeks.
