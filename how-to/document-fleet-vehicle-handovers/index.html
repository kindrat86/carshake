Fleet handovers—when a driver takes possession of a company vehicle—are a common source of disputes. Without proper documentation, fleet managers can't determine who caused damage during a shift or rental period.

Implement a 'three-scan' policy: scan at shift start before the vehicle moves, scan at shift end immediately after parking, and scan after any concerning event (near-miss, tight parking, loaded/unloaded cargo). Each scan creates an auditable trail.

CarShake's fleet mode lets fleet managers set up vehicle profiles with QR codes. Each driver scans the QR code at pickup, creating a handover record, then scans at return. The fleet dashboard shows all handover events with timestamped condition reports.

For long-term fleet rentals, schedule weekly condition check-ins. A driver who's been using the same vehicle for a month may not notice accumulating damage. Weekly scans catch issues early, making it clear who's responsible and preventing end-of-rental surprises.

The workflow in practice

  1. Before the handover: open CarShake and run the guided 8-angle scan. The app walks you through each angle so you do not miss high-risk zones like front bumper corners, rear quarter panels, and wheels. Every photo is time-stamped and geo-located automatically — no manual entry, no text overlays a user could fake.
  2. Get the receipt scanned: CarShake generates a QR handover receipt. Have the valet, attendant, or service advisor scan it to acknowledge the vehicle condition at the moment of transfer. This single step prevents most "the damage was already there" denials.
  3. After the handover: run the same 8-angle scan when you get the car back. CarShake compares it to the pre-handover scan and produces a side-by-side diff that highlights any new damage.
  4. Use the diff: if new damage is present, export the before/after diff with timestamps and geo-data. This is the document you attach to the claim, dispute letter, or insurance filing.

What most people get wrong

  • Skipping the pre-scan. The pre-scan is the entire game. Without it, the post-scan proves nothing. Sixty seconds before you hand over the keys is the highest-leverage minute in your trip.
  • Only photographing the obvious sides. Most valet damage happens on front and rear bumpers, corners, and wheels. The guided 8-angle scan exists to force coverage of these zones.
  • Not getting the receipt scanned. The QR handover receipt converts your private photo record into an acknowledged record. Operators who scan it at transfer are far less likely to dispute damage later.
  • Documenting damage after leaving the lot. Always run the post-scan immediately when you get the car back, while you are still on the property.
