How to File a Valet Damage Insurance Claim

Step-by-step: evidence, timelines, and maximizing your settlement

When a valet scratches your wheel, dents a door, or returns the car with new damage, the path to compensation runs through two parallel channels: the valet company's commercial general liability (CGL) or garage-keepers policy, and your own auto insurance. Most successful claims use both. This guide walks through every step, the evidence insurers actually accept, and how CarShake's three-stop protocol produces the documentation that shortens resolution from months to weeks.

Step 1: Document the damage before you leave the lot

Inspect the vehicle under good light before driving away. If you notice damage the moment the valet returns the car, do not pull out of the driveway. Stop, photograph everything, and ask to speak with the manager on duty. Leaving the premises gives the operator an easy out to argue the damage happened after handover. Capture wide shots showing the location of the car (signage, lot markings) and close-ups showing the damage itself.

Step 2: Notify the valet company within 24 hours

Most valet contracts require written notice of a damage claim within 24 to 72 hours; missing that window can void your right to recover. Ask the manager for a written incident report, the company's insurance carrier name, and the policy number. Independent valet operators are often uninsured or underinsured, which is why pre-damage documentation matters so much. Get the name and contact information of the specific valet who parked the car.

Step 3: Gather court-admissible evidence

Insurers and courts look for two things: proof of condition before handover and proof of condition after. Timestamped photographs from both moments establish the only timeline the valet could have caused the damage. CarShake produces both records automatically: a pre-handover scan, a QR handover receipt that proves when custody transferred, and a post-retrieval scan. Repair estimates from two licensed body shops, a copy of the original rental or parking agreement, and any surveillance requests from the venue round out the file.

Step 4: File with the valet's insurer and your own

Submit the demand letter, photos, and estimates to the valet's insurance carrier. At the same time, open a claim with your own auto insurer under collision or comprehensive coverage. Your insurer will typically pay out faster, then pursue subrogation against the valet's carrier to recover the payout and your deductible. Filing with both keeps the clock moving even if the valet's insurer drags its feet.

Step 5: Follow up and escalate

If the valet's insurer denies or delays beyond 30 days, file a complaint with your state's department of insurance. That single action prompts most stalled claims to move. For damage above $5,000 or disputes that involve total-loss valuations, a demand letter from an attorney converts the matter from ignored to settled within days.

Common pitfalls that sink claims

  • Driving the car off the lot before noticing the damage
  • Verbal-only notice to a valet attendant with no paper trail
  • Waiting more than 72 hours to report the claim
  • Repairing the damage before an adjuster inspects it
  • Relying on smartphone photos with no verifiable timestamp

FAQ

How long do valet damage claims take? Most resolve in two to six weeks when timestamped evidence is presented upfront; uninsured operator disputes can drag on for three to six months.

Will filing against the valet raise my insurance rates? Claims where you are not at fault typically do not trigger a surcharge, but check your policy's surcharge schedule.

What if the valet company says they are not responsible? Demand their written denial and policy information, then escalate to your state insurance department and file a small-claims action.
