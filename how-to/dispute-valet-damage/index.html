How to Dispute Valet Damage: The Complete Guide to Getting Paid

Quick answer: If a valet returned your car with a scratch, dent, or scrape that was not there when you dropped it off, you have more leverage than you probably think — but only if you follow the right sequence. This guide walks you through every step, from the moment you spot the damage through small-claims court.

Published 2026-07-09

Step 1: Do not drive off

Stay on the property. The moment you leave, the valet will argue the damage happened elsewhere. Take a deep breath, park if safe, and begin documenting.

Step 2: Photograph the damage immediately

Use your phone to capture the damage from multiple angles, including wider shots that show the car's location relative to the valet stand or parking lot. Timestamp and GPS metadata are critical — use CarShake or your phone's default camera (make sure location services are on).

Step 3: Compare to your pre-handover scan

If you scanned your car before handing over the keys (the 3-Stop Protocol), pull up the before images now. Side-by-side before-and-after images showing the same area clean and then damaged are the single strongest piece of evidence in any valet dispute.

Step 4: Report to the valet manager on the spot

Do not wait until the next day. Most valet operations have a limited window for incident reporting. Show the manager your before-and-after evidence. Ask them to fill out an incident report and give you a copy. Note their name, title, and the date/time of your report.

Step 5: Generate a formal demand letter

If the manager refuses to accept responsibility, immediately escalate to a written demand. Use CarShake's free claim letter generator to produce a formal letter that cites bailment law, itemizes the damage and repair cost, and sets a response deadline. Send it by email AND certified mail with return receipt requested.

Step 6: File in small-claims court if ignored

If the deadline passes with no response, you have everything you need for small claims: a demand letter that went unanswered, timestamped before-and-after photographic evidence, and the valet's incident report (or your contemporaneous notes of their refusal to provide one). Filing fees are typically $30 to $75, no lawyer is required, and the evidence standard is 'preponderance of the evidence' — meaning you just need to show it is more likely than not that the valet caused the damage.

Can I dispute valet damage after I've already left?

It is much harder. Once you leave the property, the valet will argue the damage happened elsewhere. If you have already left, gather whatever evidence you have (photos, timestamps, the valet ticket) and send a demand letter anyway — some operators will settle to avoid the hassle. But for future handovers, always re-scan before driving off.

What if the valet manager refuses to give me an incident report?

Note their name, title, and the date and time of your report. Write down exactly what they said. This contemporaneous note is itself evidence. Courts treat contemporaneous notes made at the time of an event as credible — especially when they are timestamped or stored in an app that prevents back-dating.

Is bailment law on my side?

Yes. When you hand your keys to a valet, you create a bailment relationship. The valet has a duty of reasonable care to return your vehicle in the same condition. If they fail and you have evidence of the condition at handover, the burden shifts to them to explain what happened. Read our full bailment law explainer for the legal specifics.
