CarShake vs InspectCheck

InspectCheck is a general vehicle inspection tool. CarShake is built for one job: valet handovers.

InspectCheck is a checklist-style inspection app used by rental agents, body shops, and fleet operators to walk around a vehicle, mark damage against a template, and export a signed PDF. It is a competent generalist: flexible templates, customizable damage categories, signature capture, and PDF export. What it does not have is a handover protocol — the moment where two parties exchange keys and need a shared, mutually confirmed record of who held the car and in what condition.

CarShake is purpose-built for that moment. The three-stop protocol (scan before, QR handover receipt, scan after) exists because the average valet damage dispute is not "what was wrong with the car?" — it's "whose fault is the new scratch?" That question is about custody and timing, which is exactly what InspectCheck does not capture.

When to choose CarShake

Choose CarShake when the use case is a handover, not an audit. The driver-side flow is built for someone with 60 seconds at a valet stand, not a technician with 10 minutes to fill out a form. The guided AI scan removes the decision of "what do I photograph?" and the QR-coded receipt creates a shared record both parties have acknowledged at the moment of transfer — the exact evidence missing from most valet disputes.

CarShake is also the better choice when the goal is to win a dispute, not just log one. Because every photo is stamped with an immutable timestamp and the handover receipt ties a specific vehicle condition to a specific moment of custody, the export is structured as evidence. InspectCheck produces a condition report; CarShake produces a chain of custody.

When InspectCheck might be better

InspectCheck wins when you need a flexible, template-driven inspection tool rather than a handover protocol. Body shops documenting incoming work, rental fleets doing exit inspections against a fixed checklist, and dealerships running used-car intake all benefit from a configurable form. If "I need to mark dent on the right rear door with a notes field and a signature line" describes your workflow better than "I need to prove this scratch existed before the valet," InspectCheck's generalist design fits better.

InspectCheck is also the stronger pick for organizations that already operate inside an inspection-heavy process and need their tool to bend to that process, not the other way around. CarShake's three-stop protocol is intentionally rigid — that is what makes it hold up in disputes — but rigidity is a liability when the job is open-ended documentation.

Frequently asked questions

Can I run both InspectCheck and CarShake?

Yes. A rental operator can use InspectCheck for the exit walk-around and CarShake for the customer-facing valet handover. They solve different problems and the data does not conflict.

Is CarShake really court-admissible?

CarShake evidence is timestamped, geotagged, and immutable — the properties small-claims courts and insurers look for in contemporaneous records. It is designed to be admissible; whether a specific court accepts it always depends on jurisdiction and the facts of the case.

Why does CarShake not use inspection templates?

Templates describe condition; CarShake proves custody. The 8-angle guided scan covers cosmetic condition without making the user fill out a form, which is what keeps the handover under 90 seconds.

Does InspectCheck have a QR handover receipt?

No. InspectCheck produces a PDF condition report, typically signed by one party. There is no mutually-confirmed receipt tying a specific condition to a specific moment of key transfer.
