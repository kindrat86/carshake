Overview

Rental companies aggressively pursue damage claims. This guide covers pre-rental documentation, dispute response, and how CarShake prevents fraudulent claims. Rental car damage disputes are a numbers game played at scale: the major rental companies process tens of thousands of damage claims a year, and their business model assumes most renters will pay rather than fight. Understanding how their process works — and where it breaks down — is the difference between paying for damage you did not cause and walking away clean.

How rental damage claims actually work

When you return a rental car, an attendant walks the vehicle with a tablet, flags any new damage, and photographs it. Those photos feed into a damage-billing system that generates an invoice within 7 to 30 days. The invoice typically includes repair cost, "loss of use" charges (the daily rate multiplied by estimated repair days), and an administrative fee. Many renters pay these invoices without questioning them, which is exactly what the system is designed to produce.

The weak link in this system is the pre-rental inspection. Most rental companies do a cursory walk-around at pickup and rely on you to spot and document existing damage on a paper diagram. If you do not capture timestamped photos of every pre-existing scratch, dent, and curb-rashed wheel, the company can — and often does — bill you for damage that was already there.

Step 1: Document the car before you drive off the lot

Before leaving the rental lot, walk around the car and capture a complete condition record. Photograph every panel, every wheel, the roof, the bumpers, and the interior. Note any damage on the rental agreement diagram and have an attendant initial it. CarShake's pre-rental scan automates this: a single guided pass produces timestamped, geo-located photos that the rental company cannot dispute.

Step 2: Hold onto the pre-rental record

Save the scan, the rental agreement, and any photos of the odometer and fuel gauge. Most fraudulent claims collapse the moment a customer produces a timestamped pre-rental record showing the same damage the company is now billing for.

Step 3: If you receive a damage invoice, respond in writing within 14 days

Do not call — write. Dispute the invoice in writing, attach your CarShake pre-rental scan and the signed pickup diagram, and request the company's itemized repair estimate, photos taken at return, and proof that the damage was not present at pickup. Demand a written response within 30 days. Failing to respond promptly can be treated as acceptance of the charge.

Step 4: Dispute the credit card charge if necessary

If the rental company charges your card without resolution, file a chargeback with your credit card issuer citing "goods not received" or "service dispute." Provide the same evidence package. Most issuers side with the cardholder when timestamped pre-rental documentation exists.

Step 5: Watch for loss-of-use padding

Rental companies routinely inflate loss-of-use claims by billing for days the car was not actually rented out. In many states, they must prove the car would have been rented on those specific days. Demand the fleet utilization log for the repair period.

Frequently asked questions

How long do I have to dispute a rental damage charge?

Most states allow 30 to 60 days, but credit card chargebacks typically must be filed within 60 to 120 days of the statement date. Respond in writing within 14 days to preserve all options.

What if I bought the rental company's damage waiver?

The CDW/LDW should cover the charge, but you still need to document pre-existing damage to prevent the company from billing you for prior issues after you return the car.
