Overview

Valet damage disputes hinge on proof. This guide covers documentation standards, what courts accept, and how CarShake's three-stop protocol produces admissible evidence. When a valet scratches a wheel or dents a door, the operator's first move is almost always to deny responsibility. Winning the dispute that follows is less about being right and more about presenting evidence that the operator's insurer cannot refute. This guide walks through the documentation standard courts and insurers actually apply, the defenses valet companies raise, and the evidence package that defeats each one.

What courts and insurers require

To prevail in a valet damage dispute, you must establish three things: that the damage did not exist before handover, that the valet accepted custody of the vehicle, and that the damage was present when the vehicle was returned. Each of these requires a specific type of proof. A bare assertion that "the car was fine when I parked it" carries no weight; timestamped, verifiable records do.

The most important evidentiary standard is the chain of custody. Courts look for an unbroken timeline: pre-handover condition, the moment of handover, the period during which the valet controlled the vehicle, and post-retrieval condition. The defenses valet companies raise

  • "Pre-existing damage." The most common defense. Defeated only by a timestamped pre-handover scan showing the damage was not present.
  • "The damage occurred after handover." Defeated by a QR handover receipt that fixes the moment of custody transfer.
  • "Not our employee." Independent contractor arrangements are common; the venue that hired the valet can be held vicariously liable.
  • "Disclaimer on the ticket." Rarely enforceable; courts routinely refuse to enforce one-sided negligence disclaimers.
  • "Insufficient notice." Most contracts require written notice within 24 to 72 hours.

Small claims court: when to file

If the valet's insurer denies or stalls your claim, small claims court is the most efficient path for damages under $5,000 to $10,000, depending on the state. The filing fee is typically $30 to $75, hearings happen within 30 to 60 days, and you do not need an attorney. Bring printed copies of every piece of evidence, including the CarShake scans and the QR receipt.

Common mistakes

  • Leaving the lot before documenting the damage
  • Accepting the valet's verbal promise to "take care of it"
  • Repairing the damage before an adjuster sees it
  • Letting the 24-to-72-hour notice window expire
